First steps

Quick moves for a dental emergency

Stay calm, comfort your child and call the office for guidance. For a knocked out permanent tooth, rinse it gently, hold it by the crown, keep it moist and seek care fast. For a toothache, rinse with warm water and use a cold compress. For serious bleeding, get medical help right away.

Whatever happens, a friendly voice can help you decide the next step and get your child seen quickly.

Helpful reading: the American Board of Pediatric Dentistry, the American Academy of Pediatric Dentistry and HealthyChildren.org.

Heading off the next emergency

Many childhood dental emergencies can be prevented. A well fitted mouthguard protects young athletes, and steady habits keep teeth strong enough to handle everyday knocks.

Regular pediatric preventive dentistry and routine pediatric dental checkups catch small issues before they become painful surprises.

Ways to lower the risk

  • Wear a mouthguard for sports
  • Skip chewing ice and hard candy
  • Keep up with checkups
  • Treat small cavities early
  • Childproof against falls
  • Save the office number in your phone

More from: ADA MouthHealthy and CDC oral health guidance.

Quick guide to common emergencies

A calm first response makes all the difference.

Knocked out tooth

Hold it by the crown, rinse gently, keep it moist and call right away.

Toothache

Rinse with warm water, use a cold compress and call for advice.

Chipped tooth

Save the pieces, rinse the mouth and call to have it checked.

Bumped or bleeding

Apply gentle pressure with clean gauze and seek care if it continues.

Good to know

Emergency questions

A permanent tooth got knocked out. What now?

Hold it by the crown, rinse gently, keep it moist in milk or saliva and call the office immediately.

Is a toothache an emergency?

A lasting toothache should be seen promptly. Rinse with warm water, use a cold compress and call.

What about a chipped tooth?

Save any pieces, rinse the mouth and call so it can be checked for hidden damage.

What counts as an emergency?

Knocked out or loose teeth, bad toothaches, swelling, ongoing bleeding and mouth injuries all warrant a call.

Do baby tooth injuries matter?

Yes, they can affect developing adult teeth, so a quick check is wise.
